Merchants can now say no to your card.

2 July 2026Payments · Agentic commerceSourced

Most of the coverage this stretch has been about the front of the transaction, who builds the agent, who mints the stablecoin it pays with. On 9 June a federal judge quietly changed a rule that sits underneath all of it, the rule about which card a merchant even has to take. Here is what landed, and what it means from the rails.

The interchange settlement gets preliminary approval

Judge Brian Cogan of the Eastern District of New York granted preliminary approval on 9 June to a revised $38 billion settlement between Visa, Mastercard, and roughly twelve million US merchants, closing out a swipe-fee dispute that has run for more than twenty years [1]. He called the deal "fair, reasonable, and adequate" and said he was likely to approve it in full, despite roughly forty objection letters he had not yet ruled on [1].

The part that changes checkout, not just the fee

The money side is a 10 basis point cut to posted interchange rates across the board for five years, plus a cap of 125 basis points on standard consumer credit cards for eight years [2]. But the equitable relief is the part an operator should read twice. Visa and Mastercard must rewrite network acceptance rules over the next five years so merchants can, for the first time, decline high-cost premium or commercial cards while still accepting standard ones from the same network [1]. That is a change to what "accepting Visa" actually means at the point of sale, and preliminary estimates put the total merchant savings over the life of the deal above $200 billion [1].

Where it lands on the agent layer

Adyen's head of agentic commerce put the industry at 0.5 out of 5 on maturity right now, rising to maybe 1.5 a year out, and said plainly that the AI was never the hard part, the plumbing underneath it is, meaning machine-readable catalogs, trust, fraud liability, and a pile of unreconciled protocols [3]. Card acceptance rules are exactly that kind of plumbing. Once merchants can selectively decline a card tier depending on cost, an agent doing the purchasing on someone's behalf has to know that before it presents a card, not find out at decline.

Read from the rails

Card acceptance used to be binary at the merchant level: you took the network or you did not. This settlement makes it conditional, tier by tier, merchant by merchant, and that conditionality has to live somewhere machine-readable or every failed transaction becomes a retry loop nobody designed for. In ten years running payments operations I never saw a rule change this size roll out without a stretch of declined transactions that support teams could not explain to customers, because the rule lived in a network document and not in the authorisation response. If checkout is increasingly an agent presenting a card on a person's behalf, the tier-level acceptance data needs to reach the agent before the attempt, not after the decline, or the agent just looks broken to the user who trusted it.

The other thing worth flagging: this is injunctive relief, not a settlement cheque. It changes network rules for five years starting whenever final approval lands, which the objections process could still push past 2026. Watch the ruling on those forty objections, because that date is when merchants actually get the right to say no, and when the checkout systems, human or agentic, have to catch up.

Twenty years of litigation just gave merchants the right to decline a card tier. The rule is only useful once it reaches the authorisation logic, human or agentic, before the decline does.
