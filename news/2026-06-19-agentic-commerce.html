Agents start spending: Visa, Stripe, Adyen, and a 4% toll.

19 June 2026Payments · Agentic commerceSourced

For two years agent-led checkout was a stage demo: a model that could "buy" something if every condition was perfect. This week it started to look like infrastructure. The card networks, the processors, and an open protocol layer all moved in the same direction at once, and the rulebook for the money underneath it took shape in Washington. Here is what happened, and what it means from the rails.

The processors shipped product

Adyen unveiled a suite called Adyen Agentic on 16 June, aimed at helping merchants sell through conversational AI and cover the stages of an agent-driven customer journey [1]. Stripe, meanwhile, has been building the plumbing for a while: an AI Agents SDK, single-use virtual cards with merchant and amount controls, and Shared Payment Tokens that now carry Mastercard Agent Pay and Visa's intelligent-commerce flows, plus buy-now-pay-later methods [2].

The networks picked their model

Visa scaled its agentic-commerce push through a protocol collaboration with Stripe [3] and a separate tie-up with OpenAI to put secure Visa payments inside agent experiences [4]. Underneath the brand names sits a shared idea. Mastercard Agent Pay binds a tokenised card credential to a specific agent, a specific merchant scope, and a specific consent policy. Visa's Trusted Agent Protocol does the credential-plus-authorization dance over existing web infrastructure. And an open layer, AP2, defines three mandates the networks consume as inputs: an Intent Mandate for what the user authorised, a Cart Mandate for what the merchant offered, and a Payment Mandate for settlement [5].

The toll appeared

OpenAI's Instant Checkout in ChatGPT lets people buy inside a conversation, and it introduced a new economic model earlier in 2026: a 4% transaction fee on autonomous agent-led conversions [4]. Demand is not theoretical. Visa's research has 47% of US shoppers already using AI for at least one shopping task, such as price comparison or recommendations [1].

And the money underneath got a rulebook

While the front end learned to spend, the back end got regulated. The GENIUS Act, enacted in July 2025, is now in active rulemaking. The OCC issued proposed rules on 2 March, the FDIC approved its notice of proposed rulemaking on 7 April, and Treasury's FinCEN and OFAC proposed treating permitted stablecoin issuers as financial institutions under the Bank Secrecy Act [6]. Final regulations are due by 18 July 2026, and the Act takes effect on the earlier of January 2027 or 120 days after those final rules [7].

Read from the rails

Strip the branding and the agentic-payments stack is three old payment problems wearing new names. The mandate model is authorization plus tokenization plus consent, which is exactly how a card transaction has always been scoped, only now the cardholder is a piece of software acting under a policy. The 4% checkout fee is interchange by another name, and whoever owns that toll owns the economics of the channel.

The parts I would watch are the parts the demos skip. Replay protection: an agent that retries a failed call must not buy twice, which is the same idempotency problem I have hit running my own agents. Reconciliation and dispute: when an autonomous purchase goes wrong, whose mandate was violated, and how do you prove it after the fact. And exception handling, because a migration, or a protocol, is judged not on the day it launches but on the first month of edge cases. The networks have built the happy path. The repair queue is where this will actually be won.

The mandate model is authorization, tokenization, and consent, rebuilt for a buyer that is software. The unsolved parts are replay protection, reconciliation, and dispute.
