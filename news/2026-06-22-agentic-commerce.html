The buy button was never the hard part.

22 June 2026Payments · Agentic commerceSourced

Earlier this month the networks were busy launching. Mastercard opened agent-to-agent payments, Visa tied itself to OpenAI, Adyen shipped a suite for merchants. This week the story turned the other way. The loudest signal is not a launch, it is a retreat, and what is filling the space behind it tells you where the real difficulty sits. It was never whether an agent could press buy. It was where the buy actually completes, and who carries the failures when it does not.

The leader stepped back

OpenAI scaled back the in-chat checkout it put inside ChatGPT, with the feature that let people complete a purchase without leaving the chat deprioritised after near-zero traction [1]. The numbers explain the climbdown. Around thirty Shopify merchants were live through Instant Checkout against an announced ambition of more than a million, and completing a purchase inside an answer engine turned out to be the least-adopted thing people did there [2]. Forrester's read was blunt: payments are heavy on compliance, order completion is an emotional moment, and it is easy to lose a customer's patience and trust before the thing is done [1]. The checkout is now moving into merchants' own apps inside ChatGPT, or back to their websites.

Google answered with a cart, not a button

Google took the opposite bet. At I/O it introduced Universal Cart, a single cart that follows you across Search, the Gemini app, YouTube and Gmail, finds price drops, and lets you check out with Google Pay or hand the order back to the merchant's site to finish [3]. Underneath it sits the Universal Commerce Protocol, a shared language for agents and merchants that Google built with retail partners and is rolling out across Search and Gemini in the US this summer, with YouTube, Gmail and other countries to follow [4]. The contrast is the point. One firm tried to own the final step and pulled back. The other is trying to own the cart and leave the final step where it already works.

The funding layer got wired

While the front end argued about checkout, the money plumbing kept building. Circle and Nium linked Circle's USDC settlement to Nium's payout rail, so funds can move as a stablecoin and land in local currency across more than 190 countries through a single API [5]. The framing is explicitly agentic: automated, real-time decisions still need a reliable way to move value across borders and settle it locally, which is the part an agent cannot improvise [6]. Stablecoins keep getting cast as the programmable funding layer underneath all of this, because they are digital-native and divisible enough for high-volume machine payments.

The money people are pricing it in years

For all the launches, the people holding the budget are being honest about the clock. Visa's finance chief described agentic commerce and stablecoins as investments that will not pay off in the next six months but could over the next six years [7]. That is not a retreat, it is a timeline, and it lines up with what the week showed. The infrastructure is going in now. The behaviour it assumes has not arrived yet.

Read from the rails

OpenAI's pullback is the most useful thing that happened this month, because it names the part the demos always skip. Authorising a payment is the easy 90 per cent. Completing an order is the hard 10: stock that is actually there, a delivery date that holds, a price that has not moved, a settlement that clears, and a path to put it right when one of those breaks. That is not a model problem, it is an operations problem, and operations is judged on the 10, not the 90.

Notice where the surviving moves went. Google kept the hard step on the merchant's own rails rather than rebuilding it inside a chat window. Circle and Nium spent their effort on settlement and local payout, the unglamorous bit that has to reconcile at the end of the day. Ten years in payments operations taught me that the launch slide is the happy path and the real product is the repair queue. The firms quietly winning right now are the ones building for the day the order does not go through, not the day it does.

Authorising a payment is the easy ninety per cent. Completing the order is the hard ten, and the hard ten is an operations problem. Build for the failure, not the demo.
